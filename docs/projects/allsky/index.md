# Using Machine Learning with All Sky Camera Observations
The night sky is a highly dynamic environment! Clouds, airglow, humidity, precipitation, and many other atmospheric factors can affect how well ground-based telescopes capture observations of distant objects. Astronomers must constantly monitor how these conditions change, and decide when it is safe to observe. While they aim to maximize the scientific and economic efficiency of the telescopes by operating them as often as possible, they must also avoid dangerous conditions, such as precipitation, that could cause damage.

The Rothney Astrophysical Observatory (RAO), a research facility located southwest of Calgary, Alberta, hosts three large telescopes along with a series of supporting instruments that enable observers to make informed decisions. One such device is the All Sky Camera, which points straight upwards and captures images of the entire sky using a fish eye lens. At the RAO, the All Sky Camera takes a picture every 4 minutes, regardless of time of day or atmospheric conditions, and publishes them to a live feed. It also archives them for future reference. As of 2023, the All Sky Camera has been operating for nearly 5 years, with an archive of about half a million pictures!

Although weather conditions, especially cloud coverage, are critical for safe and effective observations, they are not easily quantified. Assessing weather-related risk is therefore challenging and often subject to human bias. For example, it may be safe to operate the telescopes in 10% cloud coverage, but not 50%, yet estimating such coverage by eye is inherently uncertain.

In this work, I developed a machine learning framework to classify weather conditions in all sky images, enabling quantitative, data-driven, cloud coverage classification. Such information can empower astronomers to make risk-informed choices! To do this, I first collected a significant subset of archival all sky photos and sorted them into three classes (clear, partly cloudy, cloudy). I then built and trained a convolutional neural network to classify new images. The figure below shows an example of how an early version of the algorithm performed on such observations.

The figure above shows that the model has learned to associated image features with different cloud conditions, but more importantly, provides calibrated confidence estimates for its predictions in the blue histograms. This enables astronomers to quantify cloud coverage and make risk-informed decisions about observational risk! Leveraging machine learning technologies in creative ways to drive new data analysis in traditional fields offers a powerful avenue for generating new insights, and I am excited to continue exploring problems such as this!
